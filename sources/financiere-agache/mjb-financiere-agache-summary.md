Based only on the Financière Agache annual reports you gave me, the strongest supported story is:

**Marc Jacobs Beauty was not created as a standalone Marc Jacobs operating company; it was created inside Kendo, LVMH/Financière Agache’s cosmetics incubator, to turn fashion-brand equity into a Sephora-first beauty brand. Its apparent wind-down was likely a portfolio-prioritization decision after 2020: makeup weakened sharply, Kendo’s growth center shifted toward Fenty Beauty/Fenty Skin, and the 2021 report no longer names Marc Jacobs Beauty in the highlights even though Kendo brands remain discussed as a channel. The sources you gave do not explicitly say “Marc Jacobs Beauty was discontinued” or give a formal dissolution date.**

## 1. Why create Marc Jacobs Beauty?

The business logic was **brand-extension + Sephora distribution + incubator economics**.

Marc Jacobs already sat inside the Fashion and Leather Goods portfolio as a New York luxury/fashion brand. The reports describe Marc Jacobs as “irreverent,” “urban,” “culturally driven,” and “socially engaged,” which makes it a plausible fashion identity to translate into color cosmetics. (FA_VF-interactif.pdf)

Kendo, meanwhile, is described as a **cosmetics brand incubator set up in 2010**. By 2018 it housed Kat Von D Beauty, Marc Jacobs Beauty, Ole Henriksen, Bite Beauty, and Fenty Beauty; these brands were “primarily distributed by Sephora.” (2018-Annual-financial-report-2.pdf) This tells us the creation logic: LVMH/Financière Agache did not need Marc Jacobs Beauty to build its own retail network from scratch. It could use Kendo to incubate the brand and Sephora to distribute it.

The group also explains why this model is attractive: a broad portfolio creates **synergies**, lets the group buy advertising space at competitive rates, negotiate better department-store locations, use shared services, and invest more in design and advertising, which it calls two key success factors in perfumes and cosmetics. (FA_VF-interactif.pdf)

So the business decision was probably: **use a known fashion designer’s brand equity, plug it into Kendo’s cosmetics incubation model, and scale through Sephora rather than through costly standalone beauty retail.**

## 2. What kind of product strategy did Marc Jacobs Beauty represent?

The sources suggest Marc Jacobs Beauty was competing in the **makeup innovation** space, not primarily skincare or fragrance.

In 2017, the report says “Kat Von D and Marc Jacobs Beauty continued to expand at a fast pace,” while Fenty Beauty had just launched globally at Sephora. (FA_version-anglaise-interactive.pdf) In 2018, Marc Jacobs Beauty’s momentum was specifically tied to the launch of **Shameless liquid foundation**. (2018-Annual-financial-report-2.pdf) That matters because foundation is expensive and operationally complex: it requires shade architecture, formula performance, inventory depth, and marketing credibility. The report does not give Marc Jacobs Beauty’s standalone sales, but the fact that its momentum is attributed to a foundation launch suggests the brand was trying to grow beyond prestige color items into complexion.

This is also important for your thesis: the original brand seems to have been pursuing a more complete prestige makeup line, including complexion. That likely increased product-development and inventory burden.

## 3. What changed before dissolution / wind-down?

The biggest direct evidence is the **2020 cosmetics shock**.

In 2020, Perfumes and Cosmetics revenue fell from **€6.835B in 2019 to €5.248B**, and profit from recurring operations collapsed from **€683M to €80M**; operating margin fell from **10.0% to 1.5%**. The report says the sector was hit by declining spending from international travelers and by a decline in makeup sales overall, partly offset by stronger skincare. (RFA-2020-VA-interactif.pdf)

That is the clearest evidence for why a makeup-heavy Kendo brand would become vulnerable. Marc Jacobs Beauty was not skincare-led, and the report specifically says **makeup sales overall declined** in 2020. Meanwhile, online sales grew rapidly, and the group increasingly emphasized digital, own-site, and direct channels. 

Then in 2021, Perfumes and Cosmetics recovered strongly: revenue rose to **€6.608B**, profit rebounded to **€684M**, and operating margin recovered to **10.3%**. But the named growth drivers in the 2021 highlights are Dior, Guerlain, Givenchy, Benefit, Fresh, Make Up For Ever, Fenty Beauty, Fenty Skin, Kenzo, Maison Francis Kurkdjian, etc. Marc Jacobs Beauty is not named in that 2021 highlights section. (Financiere-Agache-Financial-Annual-Report-2021.pdf)

That omission is not proof by itself, but in combination with the earlier reports it is meaningful: Marc Jacobs Beauty was called out as expanding in 2017 and as having momentum in 2018, but by the 2021 recovery discussion, the group highlights other brands and not Marc Jacobs Beauty. 

## 4. What business decision likely drove the dissolution?

Only using your sources, I would phrase it as an evidence-based inference:

**The likely decision was to stop supporting a makeup-heavy, Sephora-first fashion beauty brand that no longer looked like the best use of Kendo’s capital, marketing, and shelf space after the pandemic changed the category.**

The evidence:

Marc Jacobs Beauty depended on the Kendo/Sephora model: Kendo brands were primarily distributed by Sephora in 2018 and 2019. (2018-Annual-financial-report-2.pdf) (FA_VF-interactif.pdf)  By 2020 and 2021, the reports say Kendo brands were still primarily distributed by Sephora but increasingly through their own sites, and all brands were accelerating online sales and digital content to meet younger consumers’ expectations.  (RFA-2020-VA-interactif.pdf)(Financiere-Agache-Financial-Annual-Report-2021.pdf)

The category mix shifted: 2020 was bad for makeup overall, while skincare helped offset the damage.(RFA-2020-VA-interactif.pdf)  Kendo’s portfolio, meanwhile, had added Fenty Skin in 2020, while Fenty Beauty maintained its appeal as a premier makeup brand and Fenty Skin generated “unprecedented buzz” during the pandemic.  

The group’s 2021 strategy was selective distribution, fewer promotions, stronger direct online sales, iconic lines, successful innovations, and high-quality digital experiences.  If Marc Jacobs Beauty was not performing strongly enough against those criteria, it would make business sense to wind it down rather than continue funding product development, Sephora placement, and marketing.(Financiere-Agache-Financial-Annual-Report-2021.pdf)

## 5. The cleaner argument for your paper

You can write:

> Marc Jacobs Beauty appears to have been created as an asset-light luxury beauty extension: Kendo provided the incubation model, Sephora provided distribution, and the Marc Jacobs fashion brand provided the creative identity. The line fit Financière Agache’s stated playbook for Perfumes and Cosmetics: differentiated brands, innovation, selective retailing, digital content, and scale economies in advertising, support functions, and distribution. Its decline appears less like a failure of the Marc Jacobs name and more like a portfolio reallocation after 2020, when makeup sales weakened sharply, travel retail declined, online/direct channels became more important, and Kendo’s center of gravity shifted toward Fenty Beauty and Fenty Skin.

The one thing I would **not** claim from these sources alone is: “LVMH discontinued Marc Jacobs Beauty because it was unprofitable.” The reports do not give Marc Jacobs Beauty standalone revenue or profit. The safer claim is: **the available evidence supports a portfolio-prioritization and category-pressure explanation, not a definitive profitability explanation.**
